My Journey through an Intolerant Education System

I felt an overwhelming sense of excitement upon my acceptance at University of Engineering and Technology(UET). I was enthusiastic about exploring new academic frontiers. However the excitement was short-lived. Externally, it seemed a promising institute but in reality, it was rotten to the core. The institute had strong ties with a controversial religious and militant leader. Bigotry and religious intolerance was a prevailing ethos while academic excellence receded into obscurity.


There are numerous incidents which when recounted to someone not familiar with such a culture, may seem unbelievable. A few marginalized individuals, who are often labeled as traitors, raise their voice against the prevailing intolerance in the academic environments. One such voice is renowned Physicist and social activist Pervez Hoodbhoy. He has condemned religious intolerance and mediocrity prevalent in numerous public sector universities in Pakistan throughout his academic career. Here, I’ll illustrate a few such incidents that made my journey through the institute insufferable.


To begin, I’ll start with a few incidents involving a teacher responsible for teaching Islamic Studies. He did not cover any substantial course content throughout the course. Rather, he was more interested in labeling those who did not align with his religious and political views, as ‘infidels’. Those who opposed this derisive approach faced severe repercussions. Some left with tarnished academic records which greatly affected their academic journey. Among them was a fellow Baloch student. The teacher showed bias against the Balochis. The student could not withstand the racism and spoke up. Consequently, he faced significant academic setbacks.


The lectures on numerical analysis deviated into sessions resembling religious sermons. Rather than instructing the subject, the teacher frequently digressed into moral and religious discussions. Only last few moments of each lecture barely constituted a semblance of numerical analysis content. His approach typically involved hastily jotting down a formula on the board, demonstrating a few iterations and concluding the class abruptly. Exams became a calculator race, favoring those with superior devices.


These incidents barely scratch the surface of what I had to bare everyday. Although there were a few voices of reason, such as Dr. Haroon Babri, who really did justice with the subject they taught. But they were very few in number. Dr. Babri acknowledged the rot within the system and lamented how even brilliant minds like Feynman would falter in such an environment.


I was disheartened by the institutional decay. As a result I sought solace beyond UET'S confines. I had a strong predilection for quantum mechanics since my high school days. I independently studied the subject and was fortunate enough to participate in building of first single photon quantum mechanics laboratory of Pakistan at Lahore University of Management Sciences--LUMS. Meanwhile, I started attending courses on mathematics and Physics at Abdus Salam School of Mathematics--ASSMS. One such notable course was on general theory of relativity taught by Dr. Amer Iqbal, a renowned string theorist. Despite its detrimental impact on my grades, the whole process of learning and working on the subject I cherished the most brought me immense fulfillment.


Subsequently, I pursued admission at LUMS, where I was able to participate in research even during my undergraduate years. It was completely contrast to my previous institute. Faculty members here were notably well-versed by the subjects they taught. Although in the same national environment, LUMS exemplified to me the essence of an ideal academic environment.
